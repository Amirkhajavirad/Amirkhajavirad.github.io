About

I work on systems that don’t behave perfectly, and try to make them reliable anyway.

Most of what I build sits at the intersection of robotics, biomedical systems, and experimental platforms, where things rarely work the first time. Sensors drift, components misalign, signals get noisy. The challenge is getting everything to hold together and behave consistently under those conditions.

During my MSc in Biomedical Engineering at the University of Alberta, I built a biomechanical testing platform for distal biceps fixation. I designed mechanical fixtures, worked with 3D printed anatomical models, and integrated everything with a mechanical testing system to simulate controlled loading, with a strong focus on repeatability, alignment, and system stability across trials.

In parallel, I’ve worked on robotic and embedded systems involving sensors, actuators, and real time behavior. Much of my work centers on integration — getting components to function together as a complete system — and debugging issues that only appear outside ideal conditions.

That process is what I enjoy most: taking something that works in theory and pushing it until it works reliably in practice.

More recently, I’ve been exploring humanoid robotics — particularly systems operating in real-world environments where uncertainty, variability, and imperfect conditions are the norm.
